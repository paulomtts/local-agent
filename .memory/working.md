A: Bringing Cherie along on bike rides can work well for a 2‑year‑old Pom, but there are a few safety must‑dos. A basket can work only if it’s designed for pets and keeps her fully secured.

### Basket vs. trailer (what I’d recommend)
- **Front/rear pet basket**: OK for short, smooth rides if Cherie is calm and the basket is rigid and stable.
- **Bike pet trailer**: Usually **safer and more comfortable** (lower center of gravity, more space, less wind/debris). Better for longer rides.

### If you use a basket, make sure it has:
- **A solid mounting system** (doesn’t wobble with bumps/turns)
- **A safety tether inside** that clips to a **harness** (never a collar—Poms are prone to tracheal issues)
- **High sides + a top strap or cover** so she can’t jump out
- **A flat, non-slip padded base**
- **Weight rating** comfortably above her weight (most Poms are ~3–7 lb, but confirm Cherie’s)

### Getting Cherie comfortable (important)
1. Let her sit in the basket at home with treats (no bike movement).
2. Short “walk the bike” sessions.
3. Very short rides on quiet, smooth paths before any real distance.

### Ride safety tips for Poms
- Avoid heat: their coats can overheat; bring water and keep rides short in warm weather.
- Watch for wind/cold on faster rides (eye irritation is common—some dogs do better with a covered carrier).
- Keep speeds conservative; avoid traffic and rough terrain.

A: That makes a lot of sense for a 15‑year‑old Pom—Nina still has bursts of power (the excited sprints and jumping into the car), but the “can’t sustain it” part usually points to reduced strength, joint/low‑back stiffness, or just overall endurance limits. The goal is to let her *participate* without asking her to do repeated high-impact starts/stops.

### How to let Nina join play with Cherie (without overloading her legs)
- **Short, structured “turns”**: 20–60 seconds of gentle play, then a calm break. Several mini-rounds are safer than one long session.
- **Keep play low and predictable**: avoid chase games and sharp pivots. Try:
  - gentle **tug** (low resistance, keep her feet planted)
  - **sniff games** / treat scatter on a rug or grass
  - “find it” with treats in a few easy spots
- **Use the environment to slow Cherie down**: play in a **small area** (hallway, pen, behind a baby gate) so it’s more social and less sprinting.
- **Floor grip matters**: on slippery floors, use rugs/yoga mats—senior legs can strain just trying to stabilize.
- **Watch for “I’m done” signs**: lagging behind, stopping/looking away, sitting/lying down, bunny-hopping, licking lips, or stiffness after.

### About those excited sprints/jumping
Since she *can* sprint and jump, it’s tempting to let her, but repeated jumping/sprinting can flare pain later. Practical tweaks:
- **Limit repeated car-jumps**: a small **folding step/ramp** can reduce impact even if she *can* jump.
- If she jumps onto furniture often, consider **pet steps**.

### When it’s worth a vet check (or a pain/mobility plan)
If you’re noticing any limping, slipping, hesitation on stairs, yelping, or stiffness the next day, it’s worth asking your vet about a senior mobility evaluation (arthritis, patella issues, low-back pain are common in small breeds). Many dogs act “fine” but feel much better with targeted pain control and/or supplements/physio.
